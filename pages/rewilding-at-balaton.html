The “Rewilding at Balaton” initiative, in collaboration with Moholy-Nagy University of Art and Design, explores how the rediscovery and use of edible wild plants can strengthen the climate resilience of local communities in the Balaton region. In a time of shifting ecological conditions, biodiversity loss and eroding local knowledge, the project combines ecological research, hands-on community workshops, and culinary experimentation to re-establish a deep, reciprocal relationship between humans and their surrounding ecosystems. By engaging directly with wild plants—through foraging, identification, and preparation—participants reconnect with seasonal cycles, soil health, and the rich diversity of local flora.

Edible wild plants—once an integral part of daily diets and traditional knowledge—are reintroduced not as nostalgic curiosities, but as living tools for resilience, nutrition, and cultural continuity. The project emphasizes the importance of sustainable harvesting, local knowledge preservation, and awareness of the ecological functions of native species. Through participatory activities, the initiative encourages people to reflect on their consumption habits, food sovereignty, and the broader role of biodiversity in supporting both human and ecological well-being. In doing so, “Rewilding at Balaton” fosters not only a practical reconnection with nature, but also a deeper ecological and cultural consciousness that strengthens local identity and community cohesion.

OUTCOME

The project is generating a growing network of local actors—farmers, chefs, teachers, and young people—who now recognize the ecological and social value of wild food. We are about to launch a new collective platform that includes a digital herbarium, community recipe collections, and new partnerships between local institutions and environmental initiatives.

IMPACT

“Rewilding at Balaton” is helping to reconnect communities with their ecological surroundings and to view biodiversity as a source of resilience rather than scarcity. The initiative strengthens local identity, increases awareness of food sovereignty, and demonstrates how cultural revival can serve as an adaptive response to climate change.
